There is a version of discipline that reads like a gym poster. That version is wrong, or at least incomplete, and probably the reason the word has become unbearable to a generation of women who actually have the capacity for it. The poster version of discipline is loud, military, and morally inflected. It says you are good if you do the thing and bad if you do not. It also fails reliably, because it asks you to override the body instead of building with it.

The version of discipline the work actually requires is different. It is not louder than the mood. It is older than the mood. It is the architecture you have already built to weather the weather of the mood, before the weather rolled in.

Mood is weather. The woman you are becoming is the architecture you build to weather the weather. That is the whole line. The weather will arrive on schedule. The architecture decides what survives it.

The architecture is built on Tuesdays. The same Tuesdays as in the previous essay. The same boring Tuesdays you do not want to do anything on. The architecture is the standing seven a.m. with yourself, the standing thirty minutes of writing nobody will read this week, the standing one call you make even though the day already has a perfectly good excuse pre-loaded for skipping it.

You will not feel like it. That is the whole point. The mood will arrive — restless, low, anxious, slightly bored — and you will not feel like it, and you will do it anyway, not because you have overridden the body but because the architecture is older than the mood and the architecture wins on the days the mood would.

— Allaia.
