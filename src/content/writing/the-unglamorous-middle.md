The before is a great story. The after is a great photo. The middle is the part nobody filters. The middle is where the woman who is going to do it is separated from the woman who keeps almost doing it, and the separation is never as cinematic as it should be.

The middle is mostly mornings. A repeat-print of mornings. The alarm going off at the same hour for the eleventh consecutive Tuesday. The journal open to the page that you wrote on yesterday. The same hot lemon water. The same walk that is no longer interesting in any of the ways walks were supposed to become interesting. The same set of three calls you keep meaning to make and keep not making. The same internet, still there, still flattering you back into the version of yourself you said you wanted to leave.

The middle is the part of the work that does not photograph well because there is no shape to it yet. There is no studio reveal. There is no apartment tour. There is no rebrand. There is a woman, slightly tired, doing a slightly inconvenient thing on a slightly less convenient schedule than she would prefer, and a list of small invisible promises she is keeping anyway because there is no audience watching.

That is the whole game.

The bar is set so low. That is the point. Showing up for the small invisible promise — not the loud public one — is what becomes the woman who eventually has nothing to prove. The promise is small precisely because it has to survive the unphotogenic Tuesday. The promise is invisible precisely because the audience cannot judge it back into a performance.

There is a version of self-development that wants this middle to be more glamorous than it is. The version that wraps it in a thirty-day challenge, a colour-coded planner, a hashtag, a transformation post. The version that needs witnesses. That version is not bad — it is just brittle. The first time the Wi-Fi goes down or the cohort thins or the seasonal depression returns, the witnesses leave and the challenge collapses, and the woman is back where she started, except now slightly more performance-injured than before.

The middle the rest of us are doing is quieter than that. It does not have witnesses. It does not have a thirty-day arc. It has a Tuesday. And another Tuesday. And on the eleventh Tuesday, when you are tired and the morning is grey and the journal looks the same, you write the same sentence anyway and you make the call you have been not making and you decide — quietly, without telling anyone — that you are not going to be the version of yourself who keeps almost doing this.

That is when the woman underneath actually shows up. Not on a deadline. Not on a launch day. On a Tuesday, when nobody is watching, when the work is most boring, when there is the least possible incentive to keep going and you keep going anyway.

— Allaia.
